\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Measuring whether policymakers use scientific evidence---and whether the evidence they invoke supports
their claims---has been limited by a costly human-coding bottleneck. Large language models (LLMs) could
remove it, but an autonomous pipeline that judges claim--evidence support with no human check is
unfalsifiable and, when claims, references, and judge are all model-generated, vulnerable to
self-preference bias. We close this trust gap by validating the autonomous judge against existing
human-labeled benchmarks for the identical task. We build an open, no-paid-API pipeline---claim
extraction, reproducible reference retrieval from OpenAlex, and stance judging---and show that a
high-capacity LLM judge attains human-level accuracy on scientific claim verification (80\% accuracy,
0.80 macro-$F_1$ on SciFact; 78\% on contested Climate-FEVER claims, where strict natural-language
inference collapses to chance), that independently developed open-source models perform the task well
above chance, and that, because the benchmark text is human-written, this accuracy cannot be an artifact
of self-preference. Applying the validated pipeline
to a decade of California legislative communication on artificial intelligence, we recover a sharp
partisan asymmetry in engagement and estimate the rate at which legislators' claims are backed by
science, with valid confidence intervals via prediction-powered inference anchored on the validated
judge. The pipeline's findings are robust to a reproducible change of retrieval engine and transfer to
a second policy domain. The result is an autonomous, open, and human-validated instrument for the
large-scale study of evidence-based policymaking.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Data and code availability}
All code, the benchmark-validation harness, the LLM-judge labels, the retrieved references, and the
figure scripts are available at \url{https://github.com/bdesmarais/ai-policy-science-use}. Every result
is reproducible from committed outputs using open-source models with no paid API. The validation
benchmarks are SciFact \citep{wadden2020fact} and Climate-FEVER \citep{diggelmann2020climate}; reference
metadata are retrieved from OpenAlex \citep{priem2022openalex}.

\section*{AI-assistance disclosure}
By design, the measurement instrument in this paper uses LLMs as components, and we disclose this
explicitly. Stance ``gold'' labels were produced by Claude Opus~4.8 acting as an in-context judge (no
fine-tuning, no API key); scalable surrogate labels were produced by open-source NLI models; an
open-source panel (Qwen2.5, Phi-3.5, OLMo-2) provided an independent cross-developer check; and the
same high-capacity model assisted in drafting and in executing the analysis. Every cited reference was
checked against a primary source. The validation against human-labeled benchmarks is what licenses
these uses; all design decisions and claims are the authors'. Earlier pipeline development was supported
by NSF awards 2318460 and 2148215.
