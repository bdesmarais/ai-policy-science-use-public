\title{\textbf{Can an LLM Judge Replace Human Coders for Measuring\\
Scientific-Evidence Use in Policy? A Feasibility Study and a Cautionary Result}}

\author{Nitheesha Nakka \and Bruce A. Desmarais \and Sarah Rajtmajer \and\\
Jeffrey J. Harden \and Frederick J. Boehmke}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Can we measure, at scale and \emph{without human coders}, whether the scientific evidence that
legislators invoke supports the claims they make? We build an end-to-end pipeline that (i) extracts
empirical claims from legislators' press releases, (ii) retrieves candidate peer-reviewed science,
and (iii) assigns each (claim, reference) pair a stance (support/refute/silent). The hard step is the
third: assessing \emph{support} has historically required scarce expert coders. We test a fully
autonomous alternative---a high-capacity large language model (LLM) as an in-context judge providing
``gold'' labels, independent open-source natural-language-inference (NLI) classifiers as a scalable
surrogate, and prediction-powered inference (PPI) / design-based supervised learning (DSL) to combine
them. We report this as a \emph{feasibility study}, not a validated measurement, because we deliberately
use \emph{no human-coded labels}: the foundations we build on (the alternative-annotator test; DSL/PPI)
formally require a trusted human anchor, which we lack, so our estimates are conditional on a single,
unvalidated LLM judge. Two results follow, and we read them cautiously. First, our \emph{zero-shot}
NLI configuration agrees with the LLM judge only weakly ($\kappa \approx 0.15$--$0.21$) and labels
most pairs ``silent''; anchoring on the LLM judge and applying PPI moves the estimated support rate
from a naive $0.21$/$0.11$ (D/R) to $0.71$/$0.54$ ``under the LLM judge.'' We argue this $\sim$3$\times$
gap is too large to attribute cleanly to NLI error: it is equally consistent with a misconfigured NLI
baseline and with the LLM judge \emph{over}-calling support---and, because the claims and references
were themselves produced by LLMs and our judge is an LLM, with \emph{self-preference} (a documented
bias) inflating the result. Self-preference and the absence of a human anchor are first-order threats,
not footnotes. Our contribution is therefore a reusable, no-API, no-RA \emph{template} for this kind of
measurement, together with a cautionary result about what it can and cannot establish on its own.
\end{abstract}
