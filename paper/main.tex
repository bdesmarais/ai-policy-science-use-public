\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Whether the scientific record corroborates legislators' empirical claims is central to the study of
evidence-based policy, yet measuring it at scale has been blocked by a human-coding bottleneck: deciding
whether a study supports, refutes, or is silent on a claim has required expert annotators. We present a
pipeline that extracts empirical claims from political text, retrieves relevant science, and judges
support entirely with language models, and we establish its trustworthiness without any new in-domain
human coding, by transfer from existing human-labelled benchmarks. The stance judge matches expert labels
on curated claim--evidence pairs and, critically, on retrieved-evidence pairs that share the structure of
real applications, sitting within the band of human inter-annotator disagreement; an ensemble of small
open-source models reaches the frontier judge with no in-domain labels. Claim extraction matches human
check-worthiness labels at the state of the art and is faithful to its sources, and model-guided retrieval
recovers relevant, DOI-grounded references, reliably rejecting off-topic matches. The
judge is deliberately party-blind. Applied to a decade of California State Assembly press releases on
artificial intelligence, the pipeline shows Democratic legislators engaging with AI far more than
Republicans, and finds that the nearest literature corroborates a majority of legislators' empirical
claims at a similar rate across parties. The result is
a transferable methodology for validating autonomous measurement of political text where human gold
standards are scarce, costly, or themselves noisy.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Data and code availability}
Code, the benchmark-validation harness, the committed judge labels and model-guided queries, the retrieved
references, the scraped release text, and the figure scripts are publicly available at
\url{https://github.com/bdesmarais/ai-policy-science-use-public}. The free
components---OpenAlex and Crossref retrieval, the NLI surrogate, the open-source panel, the
prediction-powered estimator, and all figures---regenerate from committed inputs with no paid service. The
language steps depend on a single paid frontier model, Claude Opus~4.8 (claim extraction, retrieval guidance,
and stance judging); its outputs are committed as static data and regenerate only with that model, exactly
as frozen human labels would require the original coders. The validation benchmarks are SciFact,
Climate-FEVER, and AVeriTeC.

\section*{AI-assistance disclosure}
This measurement instrument uses language models as components, and we disclose exactly which. A single
frontier model, Claude Opus~4.8, performs the three language steps---claim extraction,
retrieval guidance (targeted queries and abstention), and stance judging---used in context without
fine-tuning. Everything else is free and runs locally on a single consumer GPU: the scalable surrogate
(DeBERTa- and BART-MNLI), the cross-developer panel (Qwen2.5, Phi-3.5, OLMo-2) and their combiners, the
OpenAlex and Crossref indices, and the estimator. We also report how far the one paid component can
itself be replaced: on the judging step a combined open stack reaches it without in-domain labels
(\S\ref{sec:res-validation}). A high-capacity model also assisted in drafting and in executing the
analysis; every cited reference was checked against a primary source, and all design decisions and claims
are the authors'.
