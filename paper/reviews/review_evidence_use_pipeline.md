# Referee report: "A validated, fully autonomous pipeline for measuring scientific-evidence use in political communication"

## Summary of the submission

The authors build a three-stage pipeline that takes raw political text, extracts the empirical claims it contains, retrieves candidate scientific references for each claim from OpenAlex, and assigns each claim–reference pair a stance of support, refute, or silent. The stance step is the one that historically required human coders, and the paper's central move is to validate the stance judge against two existing human-labeled claim-verification benchmarks (SciFact and Climate-FEVER) before trusting it on unlabeled legislative text. A high-capacity LLM judge reaches roughly 80 percent agreement with the human gold on SciFact and 78 percent on Climate-FEVER, an open-source panel and two NLI classifiers do worse, and a TF-IDF baseline does worst. The authors then apply the pipeline to a decade of California Assembly press releases, report that Democrats engage with AI more often and more intensively than Republicans, and use prediction-powered inference to estimate the rate at which each party's claims are backed by the retrieved science, with the LLM judge as the gold anchor and NLI as the scalable surrogate. A second corpus of education-policy releases is used to argue domain transfer.

## Recommendation

The validation-before-trust idea is the right response to the problem the paper poses, and the OpenAlex grounding is a correct design choice. But as written the paper makes several claims that its own evidence does not support, and one of those claims (that the pipeline is open and runs with no paid API) is contradicted by the identity of the gold judge. I do not think this is publishable at Nature Communications in its current form, and two of the problems are not copyedits, they require either re-scoping the contribution or running additional analysis. None of the problems is fatal to the project. All of them are addressable. My recommendation is major revision, and I would want to see the revised version before forming a view on the venue, because the honest version of this paper is narrower than the one submitted and the narrower version may fit a computational-social-science or measurement venue better than it fits Nature Communications.

The concerns below are ordered by how much they bear on that recommendation, not by where they appear in the manuscript.

## Major concerns

*The "no paid API, runs locally" claim is contradicted by the gold judge. (Fatal as written.)* The abstract, the results, and the methods all state that the pipeline is open, uses no paid API, and that all models run locally on a single consumer GPU whose heaviest component is a 7B-parameter model in half precision. The gold judge, on which the entire contribution rests, is Claude Opus 4.8. That is a proprietary frontier model. It does not run on a consumer GPU, it is not open, and it is reached through a paid interface. The disclosure's phrase "no API key" does not resolve this; the most charitable reading is that the labels were produced through a paid chat product rather than the paid API, which is still not local, still not free, and, more damaging for this paper specifically, still not programmatically reproducible from a committed script. The consequence is not cosmetic. The paper's value proposition is that you do not need a proprietary model to do this measurement, but the only validator that reaches human-comparable accuracy is the proprietary model, and the open panel that you can in fact run for free tops out at 56 to 64 percent on the benchmarks. So the honest finding is close to the opposite of the framing: the open components do not yet reach human-comparable accuracy, and the result that does depends on a closed model accessed for pay. The fix is to stop claiming the gold is local and free, and to scope the openness claim to the surrogate layer: the NLI surrogate and the open panel run locally with no API, the gold anchor is a small number of labels from a proprietary frontier judge, and PPI propagates the expensive gold through the cheap surrogate. That is a coherent and defensible design. It is just a different paper from the one the abstract describes, and the title's "fully autonomous" and the body's "no paid API" both have to come down to meet it.

*The pipeline is validated at one step and on the wrong pairs. (Fatal as a titling and framing matter.)* The title and abstract assert a validated pipeline. What is validated is the judging step, in isolation, on benchmark pairs in which the claim and the abstract are already known to be about each other. SciFact pairs a claim with its cited abstract; Climate-FEVER pairs a claim with retrieved evidence selected for relevance. In the application the judge instead reads claims against OpenAlex keyword hits, and the paper itself reports (§2.5) that this retrieval surfaces broad topical matches rather than the specific study a claim invokes, to the point that under reproducible keyword retrieval nearly everything is labeled silent and support rates fall to a few percent. The judge's 80 percent on clean pairs therefore does not transfer to the regime in which the judge is actually used, because the validation never exercised the judge on noisy, possibly-irrelevant pairings. The paper acknowledges a "domain transfer" assumption in the Discussion, but it frames it as a question of subject matter (scientific claims versus legislative claims) when the more serious gap is the structure of the pairs (curated-relevant versus keyword-retrieved). Two of the three stages, extraction and retrieval, are not validated at all. The cleanest fix is the one the paper already gestures at and then declines to perform: take a few hundred application pairs, have a human code them, and report the judge's accuracy on those. That single analysis would convert the paper's largest weakness into its strongest claim. As it stands the paper recommends this audit to its own readers rather than doing it, which a referee will read as the authors knowing where the soft spot is.

*The headline numbers come from a retrieval procedure the paper does not specify. (Fatal until specified.)* The application reports support rates of 0.71 and 0.54, and §2.5 then says these "0.5 to 0.7 rates reflect bespoke, claim-targeted retrieval," in contrast to "broad keyword retrieval," which gives a few percent. But the Methods describe only OpenAlex relevance search, which is the broad keyword retrieval that §2.5 says yields a few percent. So the procedure that produced the central results of the paper is not the procedure documented in the Methods, and it is referred to only as "bespoke." The reader cannot tell what it is, whether it is reproducible, whether it uses a paid service, or whether it involves manual curation. For a paper whose contribution is reproducibility this is the place where the documentation has to be most complete, and it is currently absent. State exactly what generated Figures 3 and 4, give the retrieval configuration, and reconcile it with the openness claim above, because if "bespoke" turns out to mean a non-open or non-deterministic step, then the reproducible pipeline and the pipeline that produced the results are two different things.

*The instrument does not measure evidence "use." (Not fatal, but the title and Introduction overstate the construct.)* The Introduction motivates the work with whether policymakers use scientific evidence. Legislators do not cite papers in press releases, and the pipeline does not observe any evidence the legislator actually consulted. It supplies the evidence itself, by retrieving topically-near abstracts and asking whether they happen to support the claim. What the instrument measures is the rate at which a legislator's extracted empirical claims are corroborated by the topically-nearest literature that OpenAlex returns. That is a worthwhile quantity, but it is not evidence use, and the gap between the motivating question and the measured quantity runs through the title ("measuring scientific-evidence use") and the framing of the application. I would retitle and reframe around claim–literature consistency or corroboration, and reserve "evidence use" for a setting where the cited source is observed. The paper's own cautions in §2.4 (relevance-seeking retrieval bounds support from above, press releases are advocacy) are pointing at this same problem and should be promoted from caveats to a clear statement of what is and is not being measured.

*"Human-level accuracy" is asserted without a human ceiling.* The paper repeatedly calls the judge's performance human-level. Human-level requires a human baseline, which here would be the agreement rate between two human annotators on these benchmarks. That number is never reported. Agreement with a single gold standard of 80 percent is human-level only if humans agree with each other at about 80 percent; if inter-annotator agreement on these tasks is higher, then 80 percent is below human level. SciFact and Climate-FEVER both report annotator agreement statistics in their original papers, so this is available. Either report the human-human ceiling and compare to it, or drop "human-level" in favor of the defensible and specific statement, which is the percentage agreement with the gold labels and the comparison to reported frontier-LLM zero-shot numbers. The phrase "comparable to reported zero-shot performance of frontier LLMs" is doing real work in the SciFact paragraph and is the honest comparison; lead with it.

*The validation sample for the judge is small and is not comparable to the other validators.* The SciFact confusion matrix in Figure 2 sums to 66 pairs, and 53 of 66 correct reproduces the reported 0.803. The NLI classifiers, the panel, and the baseline are run on all 340 pairs. So Table 1 compares a judge evaluated on 66 class-stratified pairs against surrogates evaluated on 340 differently-distributed pairs, and accuracy across different samples is not directly comparable. The Climate-FEVER sample size for the judge is not stated at all. Evaluate every validator on the same pairs, report a confidence interval on the judge's accuracy (66 pairs puts a wide band on 0.80, on the order of plus or minus nine points), and state the Climate-FEVER n.

*PPI is anchored on the judge, not on humans, and with this surrogate it buys little.* Two things should be said plainly that the paper currently leaves implicit. First, prediction-powered inference gives valid intervals for the population rate the gold labeler would assign. Here the gold labeler is the judge, so the intervals are valid for what the judge would label over all pairs, and their validity for what a human would label rests entirely on the untested in-domain transfer in the second concern above. The CIs are not "valid" for the human-equivalent rate in the application until that transfer is checked. Second, the surrogate adds almost nothing here. The paper reports judge–NLI agreement of κ = 0.15 to 0.21 on the policy corpus and notes that NLI labels nearly everything silent, which means the surrogate is close to constant, the first variance term collapses, and the PPI estimate reduces to the judge's mean on the gold sample. The interval widths bear this out: half-widths around 0.13 imply an effective gold sample on the order of a few dozen pairs per party. So PPI is functioning as valid inference for the judge's labels on a small gold sample, not as an efficiency gain from a strong surrogate. Report the gold sample size n, and state honestly that the surrogate provides little variance reduction in this application rather than presenting PPI as the source of the precision.

*Claim extraction is unvalidated and can confound the partisan comparison.* The first stage is an LLM extracting up to ten empirical claims per document, and nothing checks whether it extracts the right claims, distorts them, or extracts them at systematically different rates from text written in different styles. If Democratic and Republican releases differ in how they phrase claims, and the extractor responds to that, then the per-claim support comparison is confounded by extraction behavior before the judge ever runs. At minimum, report extraction yield by party and a small human check on extraction fidelity. The partisan support comparison is one of the paper's headline results and it inherits whatever bias the unvalidated first stage introduces.

*The abstract claims judge transfer to a second domain that the paper does not demonstrate.* The abstract states that the validated judge transfers unchanged to a second policy domain. The education analysis (§2.5, Figure 5) shows only the front-end attention measurement, counts of releases by topic and party. It does not run the judge on education claims and reports no claim-support accuracy in the education domain. So the evidence shown is that the extraction-and-attention front end generalizes, which is a weaker and different claim than judge transfer. Either run the judge on education claims and report it, or change the abstract to say the front end transfers.

## Specific and smaller comments

*"Collapses to chance" needs to be reconciled with the class structure.* The paper reports NLI at about 50 percent on Climate-FEVER and calls this chance. For a balanced three-class task the random rate and the all-one-class rate are both 33 percent, and 50 percent is well above them. Either the Climate-FEVER evaluation is effectively two-class, in which case §2.2's statement that it uses "the identical three-way judgment our pipeline makes" is inaccurate, or it is three-class, in which case the right phrase is that NLI drops to roughly 50 percent and near the lexical floor, not that it collapses to chance. Relatedly, Table 1 reports no TF-IDF baseline for Climate-FEVER, so the actual floor for that benchmark is not shown; supply it or say why it is omitted.

*Figure 4 caption disagrees with the figure and the text.* The caption states the level varies from 0.11 to 0.79 for Democratic claims. The body text (§2.4) gives the Democratic range as 0.21 to 0.79, and the lowest Democratic bar in the figure is 0.21. The 0.11 is a Republican value. Fix the caption.

*"Fully autonomous" is in tension with the audit you recommend.* The Discussion calls a small in-domain human audit the natural way to add assurance. That is the right instinct, and it sits awkwardly next to "fully autonomous" and "no human coders" in the title and abstract. Run the audit (see the second major concern) and the tension resolves in your favor; leave it unrun and the autonomy framing reads as overstated.

*The cross-developer panel is a weaker self-preference control than claimed.* The argument that three independently developed open models agreeing with the judge rules out self-preference assumes their errors are independent. Independently developed is not independently erring: instruction-tuned models trained on overlapping web corpora and aligned with similar methods share systematic tendencies. And their agreement with the gold is itself weak (56 to 64 percent). The strong control in this paper is the one you already have, human-written benchmark text, which self-preference cannot explain. Lean on that and present the panel as corroborating rather than as an independent guarantee.

*The self-preference channel through claim text is not closed.* The benchmarks use human-written claims. In the application the claims are LLM-extracted text, and the judge may favor model-generated phrasing on the claim side even with human-written abstracts on the evidence side. The benchmark validation does not test this, so the claim that self-preference is bounded by benchmark accuracy holds for the evidence side but not the claim side. State the residual channel rather than implying the benchmark closes it entirely.

*Engagement figures lack member-level uncertainty.* The California Assembly is lopsidedly Democratic and a small number of prolific offices can drive per-document intensity. Figure 3 reports point differences with no uncertainty. Cluster by member and report intervals, otherwise "sharp partisan asymmetry" is an unqualified claim resting on unweighted aggregates.

*The repository link does not resolve at review time.* The root URL returns a 404 and the paper subpath blocks automated access, so I could not inspect the code, the benchmark harness, or the retrieved references. For a reproducibility paper the repository has to be public and the link has to work at submission, and a referee who cannot run the harness cannot verify the central claims. If the repository is private pending acceptance, say so; if it is meant to be public, the link is broken.

*Refute detection is emphasized but largely moot in the application.* The strong refute F1 on SciFact answers the worry that the pipeline cannot surface disconfirming evidence, but the application's relevance-seeking retrieval suppresses disconfirming evidence by construction, and §2.5 notes there is no deliberately disconfirming search. So the capability is real on the benchmark and close to unused in the application. Worth a sentence acknowledging that the retrieval design, not the judge, is what limits refute rates in the application.

*Em dashes.* The manuscript uses the em dash heavily as a pacing device, and in several places the dash is carrying a logical step that should be an explicit clause. Beyond house style, I would rewrite those so the connective work is visible. Two examples: the abstract's "where strict natural-language inference collapses to chance" and §2.3's "it is NLI, not the judge, that is unreliable" both compress arguments that the reader has to reconstruct.

## Closing

The project is worth finishing. The strongest version of it makes three specific changes: it tells the truth about the proprietary judge and re-scopes the openness claim to the surrogate layer, it runs a few-hundred-pair in-domain human audit so that "validated" describes the pipeline as used rather than one component on clean pairs, and it fully documents the retrieval that produced the headline numbers. With those three in hand the construct-validity and human-ceiling points become matters of careful wording rather than missing analysis. Without them, the paper claims more than it shows, and the gap is exactly the kind a Nature Communications referee is trained to find. I am happy to look at a revised version, and in particular at the in-domain audit, which is the single analysis that would most change my assessment.
