# Referee report, round 5: "A benchmark-validated language-model judge for measuring scientific corroboration of legislators' claims"

I pulled the current repository and checked the new material against the committed code and data. This report records a change in my recommendation, so I will state that first and then give the reasons.

## What changed, and a change in my position

Two things happened since round 4. First, every concrete correction I asked for is in: the "fully open" language is gone and replaced with the exact "one proprietary model, nothing else paid" scoping, the fresh-data result now leads with 0.57 and presents 0.68 only as a perfect-retrieval ceiling, the yield numbers are split into the conditional (7.1 versus 7.3) and unconditional (2.9 versus 0.9) rates with the thin Republican arm stated plainly, the check-worthiness claim is down to "state of the art" with the balanced-sample and moderate-kappa caveats, and both round-3 leftovers are fixed. I verified these in the text.

Second, and more important, the authors answered the objection I have pressed since round 2 rather than deferring it. My standing claim was that the validation rode on benchmark pairs in which claim and evidence are curated to be about each other, while the application pairs a claim with retrieved and often off-topic evidence, so benchmark accuracy might not transfer across that pair-structure gap, and that only an in-domain human audit could settle it. The revision adds AVeriTeC, a fact-checking benchmark whose evidence is retrieved from the open web, which is the application's pair structure, and which already carries human labels. I recomputed it: against the dataset's human gold the judge reaches accuracy 0.83, macro-F1 0.83, and Cohen's kappa 0.75, with the open panel clearing the floor. On the two benchmarks whose evidence is retrieved rather than curated, Climate-FEVER and AVeriTeC, the judge now agrees with the human gold at least as well as the field's alternative-annotator standard asks. That is a real and largely successful answer to the specific gap I named, obtained, as the rest of the validation was, from existing human labels.

I am therefore moving my recommendation. I no longer recommend against Nature Communications. With the three specific revisions below, none of which requires new analysis beyond one small human check, I would regard this as a reasonable NC submission whose remaining risk is a question of novelty and scope rather than of trust. After five rounds the validity case has gone from a single validated component with an honest map of the gaps to a pipeline whose every stage is checked and whose central transfer objection has been met on its own terms. The concerns that remain are narrow.

## What I verified

The AVeriTeC numbers reproduce exactly from the committed predictions and the dataset gold (0.833 accuracy, 0.826 macro-F1, kappa 0.750 on the balanced 90-pair sample), and the labels are the dataset's human verdicts, not the model's. The band figure's values are consistent (judge kappa 0.71, 0.67, 0.75 against human 0.75, 0.33, 0.62 across SciFact, Climate-FEVER, AVeriTeC). The round-4 fixes are all present in the text. The extraction and retrieval results from prior rounds still hold.

## Concerns

*The "above the human band" framing overstates a comparison the authors themselves flag as not like-for-like. (Not fatal; soften the headline to match the body.)* The abstract, the intro, and the discussion all say the judge agrees with human labels "above" or "exceeds" the human inter-annotator band. The results section then states the caveat correctly: the judge's kappa is measured against the adjudicated gold, while the human figure is pairwise inter-annotator agreement, and agreeing with a settled gold is the easier quantity. Those are not the same measurement, so "exceeds the human band" is not established by 0.75 against 0.62; what is established is the weaker and still-useful claim that the judge sits within the band of human disagreement by the alternative-annotator standard. Bring the abstract, intro, and discussion into line with the results section's own caveat: within the band, not above it. To actually claim "above," you would need the judge's pairwise agreement with individual annotators, or human agreement against the adjudicated gold, so that both numbers use the same reference.

*Retrieval relevance is still self-graded; AVeriTeC validates stance on provided evidence, not the model's own retrieval. (Not fatal, but it is now the one genuinely unvalidated step, and the fix is small.)* AVeriTeC closes the stance-transfer gap because it hands the judge retrieved evidence and asks for a verdict, which is what the application's judging step does. It does not test the step before it: whether the model's own queries surface on-topic evidence. That is the 97-percent relevance yield, and it is still labeled by the same model that wrote the queries, with no human check. This matters less than the stance gap did, and it is cheaper to close, because relevance is the one judgment on which humans actually agree well, unlike stance on contested claims. A few-hundred-item human relevance check, on-topic or not for the model-retrieved and keyword-retrieved samples, would replace the self-graded 97 percent with an independent number and would be clean precisely where a stance audit would be noisy. This is the small in-domain human check I would still ask for, and it is a far smaller thing than the full stance audit the earlier reports pressed for, which AVeriTeC has now largely substituted.

*The low-human-agreement argument is double-edged and only half-acknowledged. (Not fatal; state the other half.)* The discussion uses the weak human agreement on contested claims, Climate-FEVER at 0.33 and AVeriTeC at 0.62, to argue that a small audit would be a noisy yardstick, which is fair and well-argued. But the same fact limits what the corroboration estimate itself means: if trained humans agree only moderately on whether evidence supports a contested claim, then a reported "0.71 of Democratic claims are corroborated" inherits that construct ambiguity on exactly the contested claims that matter. The paper should say this where it reports the corroboration rate, not only where it deflects the audit. The argument that humans are noisy cannot be used to discount the audit without also being applied to the estimate the audit would check.

## On the no-human-coder defense

The defense the authors added is mostly sound, and I want to be specific about which parts. The alternative-annotator framing, is the judge inside the band of human disagreement, is the right standard and the one the field uses, and the judge passes it on application-structure pairs. The party-blind design is a genuine strength and a good argument: the judge never sees the party label, so the documented party-cue effect cannot move it directly, and any residual signal must travel through the claim's wording that a human reads too. These two arguments carry real weight. The third argument, that a human gold cannot be regenerated whereas the model labels can, is the weak one and I would drop or soften it: a validation set is frozen by nature, and the paper already rests on three frozen human-labeled benchmarks whose labels no one can regenerate either, so regenerability is not a property that distinguishes benchmark transfer from a frozen in-domain audit. The case for not gating on the audit stands on the first two arguments and on AVeriTeC; it does not need the third.

And one phrase to retire: the discussion says the curated-versus-application gap is "closed." AVeriTeC narrows it substantially and is the right evidence, but it differs from the application in domain, general fact-checking rather than legislative AI, and in retrieval noise, since its evidence was assembled to be adjudicable while the application's keyword hits are frequently off-topic, as the paper's own seven retrieval misses show. "Substantially narrowed, on the pair structure the application produces" is what the evidence supports; "closed" is one word too strong.

## What is strong

The validation is now unusually thorough for this kind of work: three stance benchmarks spanning curated to web-retrieved evidence, an extraction step checked against human check-worthiness labels and an open faithfulness metric, an open ensemble that reaches the frontier judge on the judging step, and a reproducible, DOI-grounded retrieval step that removed the second paid service. The honesty has held through five rounds in nearly every place, and the AVeriTeC addition in particular is the kind of response that answers an objection rather than working around it. The party-blind design is a real methodological contribution to the narrower problem of LLM judging of political text, and worth foregrounding.

## Venue

The trust question that kept this below the bar in earlier rounds is largely settled. What remains for Nature Communications is no longer validity but significance and scope: the methodological contributions, benchmark-anchored validation including retrieved-evidence pairs, an open ensemble matching a frontier judge, and model-guided reproducible retrieval, are solid and generalizable, while the substantive application is one chamber of one state with a Democratic-dominated partisan contrast the paper correctly calls suggestive rather than tested. That is a fair NC submission on the methods, with the application as demonstration, and the editor's judgment on novelty and breadth is now the operative question rather than a hole in the validation. With the headline "band" claim softened to match the body, a small human relevance check on retrieval, and the corroboration-interpretability point stated, I would not stand in the way. The work has earned the change in recommendation.
