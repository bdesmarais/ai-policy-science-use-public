# Referee report, round 6: "Benchmark-validated, party-blind language-model measurement of whether science corroborates legislators' claims"

I pulled the current repository and checked the new material against the committed code and data. This is a short report, because the work is close to converged and the remaining points are polish rather than substance.

## What changed

Every round-5 fix is in, and done well. "Above the human band" is now "within the band of human disagreement" everywhere, and the caveat I asked for, that the judge's kappa is against an adjudicated gold while the human figure is pairwise, is now stated in the figure caption itself, which is the right place for it. "Closed" is retired for "substantially narrowed, on the pair structure the application produces," with the two caveats I named adopted directly (AVeriTeC is general fact-checking, and its evidence was assembled to be adjudicable). The construct-ambiguity point now appears where the corroboration rate is reported, not only where the audit is deflected. The regenerability argument is dropped. Two larger things also happened: the retrieval-relevance step is now checked against human-derived labels rather than self-graded, and the paper was rewritten as a current-state report for submission, which removed GPT-5 entirely and, with it, the headline corroboration number GPT-5 had produced.

## Recommendation

Unchanged from round 5, and now I can say it without reservation: I do not recommend against Nature Communications. The validity case is complete and honestly scoped, the rewrite preserved that honesty rather than sanding it off, and the two points below are minor revisions, not conditions. The operative question for the venue is significance and breadth, which is the editor's call, and I return to it at the end. What I want to record is that the trust question that occupied the first four rounds is settled, and that removing GPT-5 resolved the last structural concern I had carried, since the headline application number now comes from the reproducible pipeline whose every stage is independently checked, not from a paid generative step the paper elsewhere disowned.

## Two remaining points

*The relevance check uses easy negatives, so 96% validates less than it appears to. (Minor; caveat it or pair it with the harder evidence you already have.)* The new check is a real, human-anchored response, and it rules out the worst failure mode: at perfect precision the model never calls a random abstract relevant. But the negatives are random abstracts, which on SciFact means a paper from an entirely different field, and the application's relevance failures are not random papers. They are topically adjacent ones, the data-center paper that is not about water, the biosecurity paper that does not bear on the specific claim, which the paper itself documents as its retrieval misses. Distinguishing a cited paper from a random one is the easy discrimination; distinguishing an on-point paper from a topically-adjacent one is the hard discrimination the application actually requires, and a cited-versus-random test does not exercise it. The honest indicator of the hard case is already in the paper: AVeriTeC's not-enough-evidence class is exactly the retrieved-but-insufficient relevance boundary, and the judge's errors concentrate there, nine of fifteen. So the complete and more cautious statement is that the model rejects obvious mismatches reliably (the 96%) but is uncertain precisely on the retrieved-but-adjacent boundary (the AVeriTeC errors), which is where the application's misses live. Present those two together, and do not let the 96% stand in the abstract as if it validated relevance on the application's hard cases. If you want to close the point rather than caveat it, the clean test is cited-versus-same-topic-noncited on SciFact, which has same-area papers to draw the hard negatives from, or a human relevance pass over the model's own retrieved pairs, whose negatives are hard by construction.

*The application has thinned to a demonstration, which sharpens the significance question. (Minor; either widen it or frame it as such.)* Removing GPT-5 was the right call and the 0.57 is better supported than the old 0.71 because every stage feeding it is now validated. But the consequence is that the substantive application is now an engagement asymmetry plus a single corroboration rate on 44 Democratic claims, with the partisan corroboration comparison correctly abandoned for want of Republican claims. That is honest, and thin. You appear to have the full historical AI corpus retrieved and the prediction-powered machinery in hand, so a model-guided corroboration estimate over that larger corpus, rather than the 44-claim current window, would give the application more to stand on; if that is not feasible, the paper should call the application a demonstration of the instrument in plain terms rather than a substantive finding. This is the one place where the work's narrowness, not its validity, is the exposure for a Nature Communications submission.

## What is strong, and a note on convergence

The validation is now unusually complete for this kind of work and, more to the point, honestly bounded at every step, with the band caveat in the caption and the narrowed-not-closed language as careful as I have seen. The party-blind design, foregrounded in the new title, is a genuine contribution to the narrower problem of LLM judging of political text and deserves the prominence it now has. Dropping GPT-5 to report the headline from the reproducible pipeline is the right scientific choice even though it cost a larger-looking number. After six rounds the substantive disagreements are settled; the two points above are revisions a copyeditor and one additional figure could absorb, and neither changes my recommendation. The methods are the contribution, they are sound, and they are now presented honestly. The remaining question is whether the editor judges the methodological advance and the demonstration broad enough for the venue, and that is a judgment I would leave to them rather than relitigate. I would be glad to see the hard-negative relevance test if you run it, but I would not hold the paper for it.
