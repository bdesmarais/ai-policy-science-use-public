# Referee report, round 2: "A benchmark-validated language-model judge for measuring scientific corroboration of legislators' claims"

I was able to clone the public repository this time and run the code and data against the manuscript, so this report is grounded in verification rather than reading alone.

## What the revision changed

The revision is substantial, not cosmetic, and it took the first report seriously on essentially every point. The title and framing moved from a validated autonomous pipeline to a benchmark-validated judge, and from "evidence use" to "scientific corroboration of claims." The abstract, the intro contributions, the methods, and the discussion now state plainly that the gold judge is a proprietary model that is neither local nor free, that only the surrogate layer is open, that the headline corroboration rates come from a proprietary generative retrieval step while reproducible OpenAlex retrieval surfaces almost nothing, that only the judging step is validated and only on curated pairs, and that the decisive in-domain human audit is foreclosed by the no-human-coder design. The validation table was rebuilt by scoring every validator on the exact pairs the judge saw, with Wilson intervals, and the honest consequence is reported: on clean SciFact pairs the open NLI surrogate is not separable from the judge, and the judge's advantage is real only on Climate-FEVER. A human inter-annotator ceiling is now reported and compared against. The claim-extraction imbalance is disclosed. This is a more honest paper than the one I first read, and I want to be specific about that because the honesty is the thing that now defines it.

## Recommendation

I recommend against Nature Communications, and I do not think the obstacle is one more revision can remove. My reasoning has changed since round one only in that the honest paper is now in front of me and the picture it draws is clear. The reproducible, open core of this work (OpenAlex retrieval with the open NLI surrogate and panel) does not work for the application: on the policy corpus it drives nearly every pair to silent and returns corroboration rates of half a percent to a few percent. The part that does work (the judge approaching human agreement, the 0.71 and 0.54 rates) depends on two proprietary, non-reproducible components, an in-session frontier judge and GPT-5 generative retrieval, and the latter is the model-naming-its-own-sources procedure the paper elsewhere identifies as the fabrication failure mode. The decisive validation, an in-domain human audit, is precluded by a constraint the project imposed on itself. What remains as the positive methodological contribution, validate an automated judge on an existing human-labeled benchmark for the identical task before trusting it, is sound and worth stating, but it is close to established practice and is in fact formalized by the alternative-annotator test the paper already cites (Calderon et al. 2025). So the contribution as it now honestly stands is a validation protocol plus a careful map of where the open, autonomous approach breaks. That is a real and useful contribution for the computational-social-science community, and it is publishable, but at a methods or computational-social-science venue (Political Analysis, Political Science Research and Methods, EPJ Data Science, or PNAS Nexus), not at Nature Communications, whose bar is a significant advance that stands on its own. None of this is fatal to the work; it is a question of matching the venue to the honest claim. The two routes that would make it a Nature Communications paper are real research, not rewriting, and I describe them at the end.

The concerns below are ordered by how much they bear on that recommendation.

## What I verified, and what holds up

I recomputed the validation table from the committed predictions. Every cell reproduces. On the 66 SciFact pairs the judge sees, NLI DeBERTa is 0.758, NLI BART 0.742, Qwen 0.697, Phi 0.530, OLMo 0.530, TF-IDF 0.455, judge 0.803; on the 45 Climate-FEVER pairs, NLI 0.489 and 0.511, Qwen 0.556, Phi 0.667, OLMo 0.511, judge 0.778. These match Table 1 exactly. The headline application result also reproduces from committed artifacts: validation_report.json gives Democratic 0.7076 with CI [0.573, 0.843] and Republican 0.5441 with CI [0.414, 0.674], anchored on 60 judge labels per party, with naive NLI estimates of 0.208 and 0.111, and the 120 judge labels are committed in claude_judge_labels.json. The two-configuration disclosure in Methods, the human-ceiling comparison, the PPI-reduces-to-the-anchor point, and the extraction-yield imbalance are all stated accurately. The numerical core of the paper is clean, and the reproducibility claims it makes about the open components are true. I note this because it means the problems that remain are about framing and a few specific statistics, not about the integrity of the main results.

## Concerns

*The "open models match the judge" claim in the abstract and intro contradicts the body and the data. (Not fatal, but it is the kind of overstatement that undercuts the paper's own credibility, which is the paper's whole stock-in-trade now.)* The abstract says open models, named as "zero-shot natural-language inference; a panel of small instruction-tuned models," match the judge on clean SciFact pairs, and contribution (2) repeats it. But only the NLI surrogate matches (0.76 and 0.74, intervals overlapping the judge's 0.80). The panel does not: Qwen is 0.70, and Phi and OLMo are 0.53, well below the judge, and the results section says so directly ("the small open-panel models trail (0.53--0.70)"). So the abstract and intro attribute to the panel a result that only NLI achieves. The fix is one clause: NLI matches the judge on clean SciFact pairs; the panel trails on both benchmarks. This matters more than a normal wording fix because the paper is now selling its precision about what is and is not true, and this is a place where the headline says something the table contradicts.

*Results section 2.5 still calls the judge "fully reproducible," which contradicts the rest of the revision. (Not fatal; a leftover.)* The robustness subsection says "the judge is fully reproducible and human-validated, so it is the retrieval step ... that must be made both reproducible and claim-targeted." Everywhere else the paper is careful that the judge is proprietary, in-session, and not reproducible, and that what is committed is a frozen set of judge labels. The committed metadata confirms this: the labels were produced "in-session, no API key." Frozen labels make the downstream analysis reproducible, exactly as frozen human labels would, but the judging process is not reproducible, and section 2.5 should say "the judge's labels are committed and frozen," not "the judge is fully reproducible." As written it reintroduces the claim the revision was built to retract.

*The κ values attributed to the panel are wrong and inconsistent between sections. (Not fatal; fix the numbers.)* The results section reports the panel's "agreement with the gold" as κ = 0.27--0.54; the discussion reports the same quantity as κ = 0.29--0.54. They disagree with each other, and neither matches the data. The actual panel-versus-gold Cohen's κ, computed from the committed predictions, is 0.29, 0.44, and 0.34 on SciFact and 0.34, 0.44, and 0.28 on Climate-FEVER, a range of about 0.28 to 0.44. The upper bound of 0.54 does not belong to any panel model against the gold; 0.541 is the DeBERTa-versus-BART agreement, an NLI-to-NLI number. So the cited range mixes an inter-validator value into a validator-versus-gold statement and reports it two different ways. Replace it with the correct panel-versus-gold range, roughly 0.28 to 0.44, in both sections.

*The headline number is produced by the exact all-LLM loop the paper names as the circularity threat, and benchmark validation does not cover that loop. (Not fatal, but it should be stated where the headline lives, not only in pieces.)* The paper's stated circularity worry is a model judging model-surfaced evidence for model-generated claims. The headline 0.71 and 0.54 are computed on LLM-extracted claims, paired with references that a proprietary model generated as claim-targeted, judged by a same-family model. That is the all-LLM loop. The benchmark validation addresses a different object: a judge reading human-written claims against human-cited or relevance-retrieved human evidence. The paper concedes the claim-side self-preference channel, and separately concedes the generative-retrieval dependence, but it does not connect them at the point where they compound. The validated quantity and the headline quantity sit at opposite ends of the paper, and the validation covers the end that is not the headline. One paragraph in section 2.4 or the discussion should say this plainly: the corroboration rate is the least-validated number in the paper, not because the judge is weak but because it is the one place all three model stages stack and the benchmark anchor does not reach.

*The headline references are only about a third DOI-bearing, in a paper that sells fabrication-free retrieval. (Not fatal; report the rate.)* The paper's reproducibility argument leans on OpenAlex returning only real, dereferenceable records and thereby eliminating citation fabrication. But the headline rates do not use OpenAlex; they use the GPT-5 generative retrieval, and in the committed Democratic reference set only about a third of the returned references carry a DOI (115 of 333 across 100 claims). The rest are not DOI-verifiable. Web-search grounding presumably reduces outright invention, but a third-DOI-coverage reference set is partial exposure to exactly the failure mode the paper claims to have closed, and the corroboration judgments are made against these references. The honest paper should report the DOI and verifiability rate of the generative references it actually used, alongside the OpenAlex contrast.

*"Statistically indistinguishable" overstates what overlapping intervals show, and the load-bearing comparison rests on 45 pairs. (Not fatal; precision.)* On SciFact the paper says the open NLI surrogate is statistically indistinguishable from the judge because the Wilson intervals overlap. Overlapping 95% intervals do not establish equivalence, and at n = 66 the comparison is underpowered to detect even a sizable gap, so the accurate statement is that the difference is not resolved at this sample size. The same caution applies more sharply to the central validation distinction, that the judge separates from the open models on Climate-FEVER: that result rests on 45 judge pairs, 15 per class, with cells such as a support precision of 1.0 from 11 of 11. The Climate-FEVER judge sample is the cheapest thing in the paper to enlarge and it carries the most weight, so I would enlarge it before resting the main validation claim on it.

## Smaller specific comments

*The public README contradicts the honest paper.* Anyone who reaches the repository before the manuscript sees a banner that the open problem "is now solved and validated" and an "autonomous, open, no-paid-API" description, directly above a quick-start that requires OPENAI_API_KEY. The README should be brought into line with the paper it now ships, since the repository is part of the artifact reviewers and readers will judge.

*The naive-versus-PPI gap deserves a sentence in the body.* validation_report.json shows the naive NLI estimate at 0.208 for Democrats against a PPI estimate of 0.708. That fivefold gap is the clearest possible illustration of the point the paper makes, that the surrogate is near-constant and PPI is effectively reporting the judge's mean on 60 anchor pairs. Putting the naive number next to the PPI number in the text would make the dependence on the judge visible rather than asserted.

*Engagement and education results are unchanged and correctly scoped.* The engagement aggregates are now presented as descriptive without member-clustered intervals, and the education result is correctly described as front-end transfer only. Both reproduce. No further change needed beyond the member-clustering refinement already flagged as a limitation.

## Two routes to the venue you want, and the one decision that controls them

The reason this is not a Nature Communications paper is structural, not editorial, so I will be concrete about what would change that, because both routes are within reach and one of them is largely yours to authorize.

*Relax the no-human-coder constraint and run the in-domain audit.* This is the single highest-leverage move and it is modest work: a few hundred application pairs from the generative-retrieval set, coded by two people, with the judge scored against them. That one analysis would validate the judge in the regime where it is actually used rather than only on curated pairs, would let you check the generative-versus-OpenAlex retrieval question against truth instead of against each other, and would let you measure whether the extraction imbalance distorts the partisan comparison. With it, the paper stops being "a validated component plus a map of the distance to cover" and becomes a validated instrument, which is the thing Nature Communications publishes. The constraint that forecloses this was set by the project, and you are the person who set it, so this is a decision you can make rather than a limitation you have to accept.

*Or solve the binding constraint and make the open pipeline actually work.* The paper correctly localizes the remaining problem to claim-targeted reproducible retrieval. Building that retriever, so the OpenAlex path returns claim-specific evidence rather than driving everything to silent, is a genuine research contribution in its own right and would let the reproducible, open pipeline reproduce the headline rates rather than collapsing to one percent. That is a larger lift than the audit, but it converts the paper's central negative result into a positive one.

Absent either, the honest paper is the careful, somewhat sobering map it has become, and that map belongs at a methods or computational-social-science venue where its value, telling the field precisely where autonomous open evidence-checking breaks and how to detect it, will be recognized for what it is. I would rather see it published there, correct, than stretched to fit a venue its honest scope does not reach. I am glad to look at the audit if you run it, since that is the result that would change my recommendation.
