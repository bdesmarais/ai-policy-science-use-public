# Referee report, round 4: "A benchmark-validated language-model judge for measuring scientific corroboration of legislators' claims"

I pulled the current public repository and checked the new material against the committed code, data, and logs.

## What changed

Three additions since the last round. First, the extraction step, which earlier drafts left unvalidated, is now validated with no human coders by three checks: the extractor's check-worthiness decision scored against the human-labeled ClaimBuster benchmark, a faithfulness test that asks whether each extracted claim is entailed by its own source release, and a yield-by-party comparison. Second, the corpus is refreshed through the most recent twelve months, to June 2026, by re-scraping both caucus archives. Third, retrieval is made index-agnostic: the same model-guided queries run against Crossref as a drop-in for OpenAlex, and the refreshed run uses Crossref to produce an end-to-end corroboration estimate on current claims. The paper now has a validation story for all three pipeline stages, which is a real and coherent advance over the round-3 version.

## Recommendation

The extraction validation is well done and mostly legitimate, and the recency and index-portability results are worth having. But the revision also reintroduces the exact failure mode this paper's whole history has been correcting: a section that calls the pipeline "fully open" with "no proprietary component anywhere" while its own sentences describe the proprietary frontier model writing the queries and judging the stance. That has to come out. And the decisive test the last three reports converged on, a small in-domain human audit, is still not done, so the new fresh-data result, for all its scale, is once again the model judging its own retrieval. My recommendation is major revision. The path to Nature Communications is unchanged and now overdue: do the human audit, which would at this point validate three things at once, and stop describing a one-proprietary-model pipeline as open. Absent the audit, this is a thorough and honest methods-and-measurement paper that belongs at Political Analysis, PNAS Nexus, or EPJ Data Science. The concerns are ordered by how much they bear on that.

## What I verified, and what holds up

The new numbers trace to committed artifacts. Check-worthiness: on a balanced 150-sentence ClaimBuster sample the extractor scores accuracy 0.767, F1 0.741, precision 0.833, recall 0.667, against the open panel (Phi-3.5 F1 0.625) and the lexical floor (0.555), matching the paper. The gold here is ClaimBuster's human labels and the predictions are the model's, so this is genuine benchmark transfer, not self-grading. Faithfulness: 95.2 percent of 785 extracted claims are entailed by their source release, computed with the open DeBERTa NLI model, not the judge, with the judge only spot-checking the lowest-entailment cases; the by-party split (95.3 versus 93.2) matches. Fresh corroboration: 25 of 44 claims supported, 0.568 with Wilson interval [0.42, 0.70], backend Crossref, Democratic-only, matching the figure. These hold up, and the use of human ClaimBuster labels and an open faithfulness metric is the right way to have done the extraction checks.

## Concerns

*The refresh section claims an open pipeline it does not have, contradicting the rest of the paper. (Not fatal, but it is the single most important thing to fix, because it is the precise overclaim the whole revision history has been removing.)* Section~\ref{sec:res-refresh} is titled "no proprietary component anywhere," states the result is "reproduced on current data with an entirely open instrument," and labels the figure "the fully-open pipeline." Its own body then says "the same frontier model writes a claim-targeted query... and the benchmark-validated judge reads the best on-topic result," and the committed provenance confirms both: the queries in `ai_claim_queries.json` were generated by Claude Opus 4.8, and the judge is Claude. So the "fully open" pipeline uses a proprietary frontier model for extraction, query generation, and judging. This directly contradicts the abstract ("the gold judge is proprietary"), the methods ("we are explicit that this is a proprietary frontier model"), and the careful "no second paid service, not no proprietary model" framing the paper builds its credibility on. The accurate claim is the one the body half-states: no proprietary retrieval service, no GPT-5, no second paid service, and a free, DOI-grounded index, with the single frontier model still doing the three language steps. Change the title, the "entirely open instrument," and the "fully-open pipeline" caption to that. As written this is a self-inflicted contradiction that a referee will read as the overclaiming returning under a new heading.

*The decisive validation is still missing, and the refresh does not supply it. (Not fatal, but it is the binding limitation and it has been for four rounds.)* The refresh adds scale, recency, and index-portability, all of which are real. It does not add validity. The fresh corroboration rate of 0.57 is the validated judge reading references that the same model retrieved, scored against no human labels, so it inherits both open questions the last report named: the judge is exercised on application pairings rather than the curated pairs it was validated on, and the retrieval relevance that gates it is still self-assessed. The pattern across four rounds is consistent: each gap is met with an ingenious no-human-coder substitute, benchmark transfer for the judge, benchmark transfer for the extractor, model self-assessment for retrieval, prediction-powered inference for the estimate, and each substitute is sound for what it covers and silent on what it does not. The one check that would close the stance-transfer question and the retrieval-relevance question together is a few hundred application pairs coded by people. The design forecloses it by choice, and the paper is honest that this is the decisive missing test, but four rounds of substitutes do not add up to the test, and the substitutes are now exhausted. This is the move that would change the venue verdict, and it is cheaper than any of the three additions in this revision.

*The fresh 0.68 figure drops unfavorable cases identified after the fact. (Moderate; lead with 0.57.)* The paper reports 0.57 raw and 0.68 "excluding seven retrieval misses," where the seven are silents reclassified post hoc as retrieval failures because "the same data-center-water claim is supported when the query lands," that is, after re-querying until support appears. Dropping the cases where the open retrieval failed, having re-run them until it succeeded, is favorable selection and biases the rate upward; 0.68 is not a corroboration rate but a corroboration-rate-among-claims-the-retriever-happened-to-serve. Report 0.57 as the number and present 0.68, if at all, explicitly as the ceiling under perfect retrieval, not as a co-equal estimate.

*The Republican arm has thinned to the point where the partisan comparison is effectively Democratic-only. (Moderate; report it as such and reconcile the yield numbers.)* The yield-neutrality result rests on six Republican releases (44 claims over 6 releases gives the 7.3), and the refreshed window contains zero Republican AI releases. So "party-balanced yield, 7.1 versus 7.3" is a statement about six releases, and the partisan corroboration contrast is carried by a token Republican sample. The paper acknowledges the window is Democratic-only, which is good, but the yield-neutrality claim is presented as reassurance it cannot bear at n=6. Separately, the reconciliation with the earlier figure needs care: the paper calls the old 1.4-versus-2.4 a "mis-estimate," but the old number had Republicans higher (2.4 > 1.4) while the new narrative says Republican releases more often yield zero, which would put Republicans lower unconditionally. Report the unconditional yields explicitly and state the relationship to the conditional 7.1/7.3, rather than labeling the prior figure a mistake, since conditional and unconditional yield are different quantities and the new explanation points in the opposite direction from the old number.

## Smaller specific comments

The check-worthiness result is described as reaching "the human ceiling" in the results text, but the human inter-annotator agreement on ClaimBuster is not computed here, and the judge's own agreement is κ=0.53, which is moderate, not ceiling. The balanced-sample F1 of 0.74 is also not directly comparable to a CheckThat! shared-task state of the art reported on the natural, heavily imbalanced distribution. The abstract's and intro's "at the published state of the art" is the defensible phrasing; bring the results sentence ("at the human ceiling") into line with it, and note that the comparison is on a balanced sample.

The two leftovers from round 3 are still in the text. The results section still says "the two retrieval configurations" where the methods now describe three, and the validators subsection still ends with "All models run locally on a single consumer GPU," which reads as including the proprietary judge the same paragraph has just excluded. These were flagged last round; they are one-word fixes.

## What is genuinely strong

The extraction validation is the right response to a gap I and the authors had both flagged, and it is executed with the discipline the rest of the paper has earned: check-worthiness against ClaimBuster's human labels rather than self-assessment, faithfulness via an open NLI model rather than the judge, and a cross-developer panel that clears the floor as a competence control. The finding that the extractor is conservative, high precision and lower recall, is the honest and correct reading, and it defuses the fabrication worry directly. The index-portability result is also a real robustness contribution: showing the model-guided queries work against Crossref as well as OpenAlex removes a single-index dependency and, incidentally, demonstrates the retrieval method is not tied to one service. And keeping the corpus current to June 2026 is more than most pipelines bother to do. None of this is in question; it is the open-pipeline language and the missing human audit that are.

## Venue, and the one move that settles it

After four rounds the paper has validated every stage it can validate without people, and it has reached the limit of that approach. The remaining validity questions, stance transfer to application pairs and the relevance of model-guided retrieval, are exactly the ones benchmark transfer and self-assessment cannot answer, and the periodic slide into "fully open" language is the other symptom of the same root cause: a reluctance to either do the human audit or call the one paid model what it is. A few hundred application pairs coded by two people would now do triple duty, validating the judge where it is used, replacing the self-graded retrieval relevance with an independent number, and checking that the at-scale Crossref retrieval is relevant rather than merely DOI-bearing. With that audit and the open-pipeline language corrected to "no second paid service," the paper becomes a validated, mostly-open, reproducible instrument with all three stages checked, which is the Nature Communications threshold. Without the audit it remains a careful and now unusually complete methods-and-measurement contribution, and it should go to a venue that will value that. The constraint that forecloses the audit is the project's own, and it is the last thing standing between this work and the claim it wants to make. I would be glad to review the audit; it is the result that would change my recommendation.
