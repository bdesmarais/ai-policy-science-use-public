\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We set out to measure, at scale and without human coders, whether the scientific evidence legislators
invoke supports the claims they make, and to do so in a way that can be \emph{trusted}. The key result
is that the hardest step---judging claim--evidence support---can be performed autonomously and
validated against human judgment by transfer from established benchmarks. The LLM judge reaches
human-level accuracy on scientific claim verification, an independent panel of open models from
different developers agrees with it, and because the benchmark text is human-written, this accuracy
cannot be an artifact of self-preference. The pipeline therefore does what an unvalidated autonomous
measurement cannot: it earns trust.

\paragraph{Resolving the circularity threat.} The most serious objection to an all-LLM measurement
loop is that a model judging model-surfaced evidence may exhibit self-preference rather than assess
science \citep{panickssery2024llm}. Benchmark validation defuses this directly. SciFact and
Climate-FEVER claims and evidence are written by humans, so a judge that labels them correctly is
reading the science, not recognizing its own prose; and the agreement of three independently developed
open models with the high-capacity judge shows the signal is not idiosyncratic to one model family.
Self-preference may still operate at the margin on model-generated policy text, but it is no longer an
unfalsifiable confound---it is bounded by measured benchmark accuracy.

\paragraph{What validation does and does not cover.} Our anchor is task validity, established on two
benchmarks chosen to bracket the policy setting in difficulty and register. The residual assumption is
domain transfer: that accuracy on expert and contested scientific claims carries to legislative claims.
We regard this as reasonable---the cognitive task is identical and the two benchmarks span a wide
difficulty range---but not eliminated, and it is the natural place for a small in-domain human audit to
add further assurance. This is a far narrower and more honest caveat than ``the judge is unvalidated.''

\paragraph{A reusable instrument for computational social science.} The contribution generalizes beyond
AI policy. Many questions in the study of political communication---does a campaign's factual claim hold
up, is a talking point supported by research, how does evidence quality vary across actors---reduce to
the same claim--evidence judgment we validate here. Our finding that an open, no-API pipeline, anchored
by benchmark validation and combined with prediction-powered inference, yields trustworthy population
estimates lowers the cost and raises the credibility of such measurement. The reproducible-retrieval
choice matters too: grounding references in an open bibliographic index makes the evidence base
auditable and the pipeline exactly replicable, in contrast to generative ``retrieval'' that can
fabricate sources.

\paragraph{Diagnose across methods.} A practical lesson concerns NLI as a stand-alone measure. On
clean benchmark pairs our zero-shot NLI classifiers are strong, but on the policy corpus they label
most relevance-retrieved references ``silent,'' because an abstract rarely \emph{entails} a
press-release claim in the strict textual sense even when it substantiates it. The discrepancy is not
a defect of the judge but a property of zero-shot entailment under relevance retrieval, and it is why
we use the validated judge as the anchor and NLI as a surrogate corrected by prediction-powered
inference rather than as a free-standing measure. Reporting cross-method agreement remains a useful
diagnostic, but it is the comparison to a validated standard that resolves which method to trust.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Three limitations temper the substantive findings. (1) \emph{Domain transfer},
discussed above. (2) \emph{Retrieval is the binding constraint}: stance is assessed only over retrieved
references with no deliberately disconfirming search, so support rates are an upper and refute rates a
lower bound; more importantly, the measured level is highly sensitive to retrieval precision---broad
open-index keyword retrieval surfaces little claim-specific evidence and drives nearly all pairs to
\textsc{silent} (\S\ref{sec:res-robustness})---so a reproducible, claim-targeted retriever is the single
most valuable component to add, and the released pipeline isolates it as such. (3) \emph{Strategic text}: press
releases are advocacy, so a high support rate reflects selective citation as much as evidence quality,
and our estimates describe how well \emph{chosen} claims are backed, not balanced evidence use. The
political application is also one chamber of one state; the pipeline is state-agnostic and validated,
so multi-state deployment is a matter of corpus assembly rather than method.

\paragraph{Ethics.} Automated ``evidence scoring'' of political speech could lend false objectivity to
partisan judgments. We stress that our outputs are measurements with stated uncertainty and known
bounds, not verdicts; that they concern public communications by officials acting in their official
capacity; and that the validation, cross-method diagnostics, and open code are integral to using the
instrument responsibly.
