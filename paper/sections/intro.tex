\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Whether policymakers use scientific evidence, and whether the evidence they invoke actually supports
the claims they make, is a central question for the study of evidence-based policymaking
\citep{oliver2022works,bogenschneider2019revisiting,weiss1979many}. Answering it at scale has been
blocked by a measurement bottleneck. Establishing that a given scientific study \emph{supports},
\emph{refutes}, or is \emph{silent} on a specific empirical claim in a press release, floor speech, or
bill is a judgment that has historically required trained human coders reading both the claim and the
underlying science \citep{grimmer2013text}. Expert coding is slow, expensive, and hard to reproduce,
so studies of evidence use have been confined to small samples or coarse proxies, and cannot keep pace
with the volume of modern political communication.

Large language models (LLMs) appear to dissolve this bottleneck. Across political science and
computational social science, instruction-tuned LLMs now match or exceed trained human coders on stance
detection, framing, and other annotation tasks, at a fraction of the cost
\citep{gilardi2023chatgpt,ziems2024can,heseltine2024llm,tornberg2025llm}. This raises the possibility of
a \emph{fully autonomous} measurement pipeline---one that ingests raw political text, extracts the
empirical claims, retrieves the relevant science, and judges whether that science supports each
claim---with no human annotators and no proprietary, paid services in the loop.

The obstacle is not building such a pipeline but \emph{trusting} it. If the model that judges
claim--evidence support is never checked against a human standard, its outputs are unfalsifiable: a
fluent label is not necessarily a correct one \citep{bender2021stochastic}, and the statistical
machinery for turning noisy machine labels into valid population estimates---design-based supervised
learning and prediction-powered inference \citep{egami2023dsl,angelopoulos2023ppi}, and the
alternative-annotator test that licenses replacing a human coder \citep{calderon2025alttest}---is all
defined relative to trusted human ground truth. A second, subtler threat is circularity: when the
claims, the retrieved references, and the judge are \emph{all} produced by language models, an LLM
judge may rate model-surfaced evidence as supportive because LLM evaluators are known to prefer
model-generated and low-perplexity text---a documented \emph{self-preference} bias
\citep{panickssery2024llm,zheng2023judging}. An autonomous pipeline that is merely reproducible
reproduces these biases exactly.

Here we show that this trust gap can be closed without collecting new human annotations in the target
domain, by \emph{validating the autonomous judge against existing human-labeled benchmarks for the
identical task}. Scientific claim verification---deciding whether an abstract supports, refutes, or is
neutral toward a claim---is precisely the support-assessment step of our pipeline, and it has mature,
expert-annotated benchmarks: SciFact \citep{wadden2020fact} for expert scientific claims and
Climate-FEVER \citep{diggelmann2020climate} for contested, real-world claims phrased in lay language.
A judge that reaches human-level accuracy on these benchmarks has demonstrably learned the task; and
because the benchmark text is human-written rather than model-generated, strong accuracy there cannot
be an artifact of self-preference. Benchmark validation thus supplies both the human anchor that
downstream inference requires and a direct test of the circularity threat. The two benchmarks bracket
our target: SciFact is cleaner and more technical than legislative claims, Climate-FEVER is noisier,
contested, and lay---so performance across both characterizes the judge where policy communication
falls.

Using this strategy we build and validate a fully autonomous, open, no-paid-API pipeline for measuring
scientific-evidence use in political communication, and apply it to a decade of legislative
communication on artificial intelligence (AI)---a domain where appeals to evidence are common and
consequential. Our contributions are: (1) a reproducible pipeline---claim extraction, reference
retrieval from the open OpenAlex index \citep{priem2022openalex}, and stance judging---that runs end to
end with open-source models and a single high-capacity LLM judge, with no human coders and no paid
API; (2) human-anchored validation showing that the LLM judge reaches human-level accuracy on two
scientific-claim-verification benchmarks, that a panel of independent open-source models from different
developers agrees with it, and that self-preference does not explain the result; (3) an application to
California legislative communication on AI that recovers a sharp partisan asymmetry in engagement and
estimates the rate at which legislators' empirical claims are backed by science; and (4) evidence that
the pipeline generalizes---robust to a reproducible change of retrieval engine and transferable to a
second policy domain. The result is a measurement instrument for evidence-based policymaking that is
autonomous, open, and---critically---validated against human judgment.
