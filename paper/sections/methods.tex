\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

Our goal is a measurement procedure that maps each (claim, scientific reference) pair to a stance
label---\textsc{support}, \textsc{refute}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{silent} (irrelevant or not
addressed)---and that aggregates these labels into valid, party-level estimates of evidence use,
\emph{without} requiring human coders. We first describe the upstream pipeline that produces the
claim--reference pairs (\S\ref{sec:methods-pipeline}), then the autonomous validation framework
that is our central contribution (\S\ref{sec:methods-validation}).

\subsection{The policy-to-science pipeline}
\label{sec:methods-pipeline}

The pipeline converts a policymaking document into a set of \emph{(claim $\rightarrow$ candidate
evidence)} records in three stages.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Claim extraction.} Each press release is processed by a large language model
  prompted to extract up to ten empirical claims ``for which, if you were writing a scientific
  paper, you would need a reference.'' Output is structured (press-release id, claim number, claim
  text), isolating the verifiable propositions from rhetorical or procedural text
  \citep{grimmer2013representational}.
  \item \textbf{Evidence retrieval.} For each claim, a web-connected LLM returns up to ten
  candidate scientific references as structured JSON (title, abstract, authors, year, venue, DOI,
  URL, open-access flag), with de-duplication by DOI (else title$+$year). This stage answers
  ``what science is \emph{relevant} to this claim,'' a task at which current models are
  strong but which does not, by itself, establish \emph{support}.
  \item \textbf{Support assessment.} Each (claim, reference) pair is assigned a stance label. This
  is the step that previously required expert coders and is the focus of \S\ref{sec:methods-validation}.
\end{enumerate}
The substantive object of interest is the \emph{support rate}: among relevant claim--reference
pairs, the share for which the scientific record supports the legislator's claim, and how this
varies by party.

\subsection{Autonomous validation: LLM judge $+$ NLI surrogate $+$ PPI}
\label{sec:methods-validation}

\paragraph{Validators.} We treat stance assignment as an annotation problem and replace human
annotators with two kinds of automated \emph{validators} that fail in different ways, so that
their agreement is informative \citep{zheng2023judging}.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{LLM judge.} A high-capacity instruction-tuned model (here Claude Opus~4.8, queried
  in context with no fine-tuning and no API key) reads the claim and the reference
  title$+$abstract and returns a stance label with a brief rationale. LLMs of this class match or
  exceed trained crowd coders on relevance and stance tasks \citep{gilardi2023chatgpt,ziems2024can}.
  \item \textbf{NLI classifiers.} Two open-source natural-language-inference models with different
  architectures and training data---\texttt{DeBERTa-v3-base-mnli-fever-anli} and
  \texttt{bart-large-mnli}---score each pair with the reference as premise and the claim as
  hypothesis. We map \textsc{entailment}$\rightarrow$\textsc{support},
  \textsc{contradiction}$\rightarrow$\textsc{refute}, and \textsc{neutral}$\rightarrow$\textsc{silent}
  \citep{yin2019benchmarking,laurer2024less}. NLI is the standard discriminative approach to
  scientific claim verification \citep{wadden2020fact,koneru2024llm}.
  \item \textbf{Dependency-free baselines.} TF--IDF cosine relevance and a negation-aware lexical
  rule provide validators that run with no models at all, for continuous-integration and
  no-dependency reproduction.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Agreement as autonomous reliability.} For any pair of validators we report percent
agreement and Cohen's $\kappa$ over the shared, non-abstaining items. This plays the role that
inter-annotator reliability plays in human coding. Following the \emph{alternative-annotator
test} \citep{calderon2025alttest}, a validator is admissible as a stand-in only if it is at least
as good a predictor of the reference standard as the reference annotators are of one another;
agreement that is high \emph{among methodologically similar} validators but low \emph{across}
methods is diagnostic of a shared blind spot rather than of correctness.

\paragraph{Valid estimation under imperfect labels.} Plugging machine labels directly into a
downstream analysis biases estimates and invalidates confidence intervals, even at 80--90\%
label accuracy \citep{egami2023dsl}. We therefore treat the cheap, scalable NLI labels as a
\emph{surrogate} available for all $N$ pairs, and the LLM-judge labels as \emph{gold} available
for a sample of $n \ll N$ pairs, and combine them with prediction-powered inference
\citep{angelopoulos2023ppi}. For the binary support indicator, the PPI point estimate of the
population support rate is
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\theta}_{\text{PPI}} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)}_{\text{surrogate mean (all pairs)}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\bigl(y_j - f(x_j)\bigr)}_{\text{rectifier (gold sample)}},
  \label{eq:ppi}
\end{equation}
where $f(x_i)\in\{0,1\}$ is the NLI ``support'' prediction and $y_j\in\{0,1\}$ is the LLM-judge
gold label, with variance $\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}=\mathrm{Var}\!\big(f(x)\big)/N + \mathrm{Var}\!\big(y-f(x)\big)/n$
and a Wald 95\% interval $\hat{\theta}_{\text{PPI}}\pm 1.96\,\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}^{1/2}$. When the
rectifier is non-zero---i.e.\ when the surrogate is biased relative to the judge---PPI corrects the
naive surrogate mean while retaining the efficiency gains of labeling all $N$ pairs. Equation
\eqref{eq:ppi} is the prediction-powered special case of the design-based supervised learning
estimator \citep{egami2023dsl}; both are formally valid when the gold labels are sampled by a
known design, and both reduce to the human-coded estimate as $n\to N$.

\paragraph{Why this is ``autonomous''---and what that costs.} Every component runs locally with no
human annotation and no paid API: the LLM judge stands in for the human RA, the NLI surrogate supplies
scale, and PPI combines them. But ``autonomous'' is not free. The PPI/DSL guarantee
\citep{angelopoulos2023ppi,egami2023dsl} holds only when the anchor labels are \emph{trusted ground
truth} sampled by a known design, and the alternative-annotator test \citep{calderon2025alttest} that
licenses replacing human coders is \emph{defined} as a comparison to multiple human annotators. We have
neither: our anchor is a single, unvalidated LLM. Our confidence intervals are therefore
\emph{conditional} on that judge---they quantify sampling of the judge's labels, not error relative to
truth---and should be read as illustrative, not as validated population inference. We treat this as the
central limitation of the design, not a detail, and return to it in \S\ref{sec:discussion}. The moment a
small set of \emph{human}-verified labels becomes available, it can replace (or augment) the LLM-judge
anchor to obtain a conventionally valid estimate.

\paragraph{Threats specific to an all-LLM loop.} Two further issues follow from doing everything with
language models. First, \emph{self-preference}: LLM judges favor model-generated and low-perplexity text
and can recognize their own outputs \citep{panickssery2024llm,zheng2023judging}; because the claims and
the retrieved references in our pipeline are themselves LLM-produced, the judge may rate them as
supportive partly for that reason, biasing the support rate upward. Second, \emph{retrieval bias}: the
retrieval step seeks \emph{relevant} references and performs no deliberately disconfirming search, so a
pair scored ``support'' is supported \emph{by what an LLM chose to surface}, and the refute rate is a
lower bound. We de-duplicate identical (claim, reference) pairs before analysis, and we flag that some
retrieved ``references'' are bill text or government pages rather than peer-reviewed science, which a
faithful stance step should treat as out of scope.

\paragraph{Implementation.} The pipeline and validator are released at
\url{https://github.com/bdesmarais/ai-policy-science-use} (\texttt{scripts/core/autonomous\_validation.py});
all results below are reproducible from the committed reference outputs with a single command and
no API key.
