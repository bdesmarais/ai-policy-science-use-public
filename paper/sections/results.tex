\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{The pipeline}
We instantiate the pipeline of \S\ref{sec:methods} end to end with no human coders. Claim extraction
turns each legislative document into a set of empirical claims; for each claim we retrieve candidate
references by model-guided search over a free index (\S\ref{sec:methods-pipeline}); and each (claim,
reference) pair is assigned a stance---\textsc{support}, \textsc{refute}, or \textsc{silent}---by a
high-capacity LLM judge, a panel of three open-source models, and two natural-language-inference (NLI)
classifiers. The open components---the NLI surrogate, the panel, OpenAlex retrieval, the
estimator---run locally on a single consumer GPU with no paid service; the high-capacity judge is a
frontier model (Claude Opus~4.8) and is not one of them (\S\ref{sec:methods-validators}). The
substantive question---what fraction of legislators' empirical claims the topically-nearest literature
\emph{corroborates}---reduces to aggregating these stance labels with valid uncertainty, which in turn
requires that the judge be trustworthy. We establish how trustworthy, and on what, next.

\subsection{A frontier judge approaches the human ceiling; open models lag on the hard task}
\label{sec:res-validation}
We score every validator against the expert-human gold on two scientific-claim-verification benchmarks
that bracket the policy setting: SciFact \citep{wadden2020fact}, expert-written claims paired with their
cited abstracts, and Climate-FEVER \citep{diggelmann2020climate}, contested real-world claims in lay
language paired with retrieved evidence. Both use the identical three-way
\textsc{support}/\textsc{refute}/\textsc{silent} judgment our pipeline makes. To keep the comparison
like-for-like, Table~\ref{tab:validation} reports accuracy with Wilson $95\%$ intervals on the
\emph{same} pairs the frontier judge labelled ($n=66$ and $45$); the judge sample is small by design,
mirroring its gold-on-a-sample role, so the intervals are wide and we read them accordingly
(Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}).

On clean SciFact pairs the frontier judge reaches $0.80$ accuracy $[0.69, 0.88]$, but the open NLI
surrogate is \emph{not separated} from it at this sample size ($0.76$ and $0.74$ vs.\ $0.80$, intervals
overlapping)---with $n=66$ the comparison is underpowered to resolve even a sizeable gap, so this is
non-resolution, not demonstrated equivalence;
the small open-panel models trail ($0.53$--$0.70$) and the TF--IDF floor is $0.44$. In Cohen's $\kappa$
against the gold the judge scores $0.71$---just below the $\kappa=0.75$ agreement SciFact reports among
its expert human coders \citep{wadden2020fact}, so ``approaching human agreement,'' not ``human-level,''
is the accurate phrase. The judge's \textsc{refute} detection is strong (precision $0.85$--$1.0$ across
benchmarks), answering the worry that the pipeline cannot recognise disconfirming evidence---though, as
\S\ref{sec:res-application} notes, retrieval rarely surfaces such evidence in the application.

The judge's advantage appears on Climate-FEVER, the benchmark closest to legislative communication.
There it holds at $0.78$ accuracy $[0.64, 0.87]$ while the NLI classifiers fall to $0.49$--$0.51$, barely
above the $0.34$ TF--IDF/chance floor for this three-class task and far below the judge (intervals do not
overlap). Strict entailment between a lay claim and a noisy evidence sentence is uninformative---exactly
the regime the policy corpus occupies. Tellingly, human annotators themselves agree only weakly on
Climate-FEVER (Krippendorff's $\alpha=0.33$; \citealp{diggelmann2020climate}), and the judge agrees with
the aggregated gold at $\kappa=0.67$, at least as consistently as the humans agree with one another; it
also handles the off-topic \textsc{not-enough-info} class well (recall $0.87$), which is the
loosely-related-pairing regime the application produces. This is why we anchor estimation on the judge
and treat NLI as a surrogate to be corrected (\S\ref{sec:methods-ppi})---and equally why the open-only
configuration is not yet a substitute for the judge on hard, policy-like claims. We flag, however, that
this central distinction rests on a small judge sample ($n=45$, $15$ per class, with cells as thin as a
support precision of $1.0$ from $11/11$); enlarging the Climate-FEVER judge set is the cheapest way to
firm up the validation claim that carries the most weight, and we mark it as the first robustness step.

\paragraph{Combining the open models closes much of the gap.} The single open models lag the judge, but
they fail in \emph{different} ways---NLI is strong on clean SciFact and collapses on lay Climate-FEVER,
the small panel is the reverse---so an ensemble can recover what any one member misses
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ensemble}). Combining the five open models with \emph{no new in-domain labels} does
exactly this. A label-free majority vote already beats every single open model on SciFact ($0.74$ full
accuracy). A \emph{stacker} trained only on the \emph{public} benchmark gold (five-fold out-of-fold, with
text features fit inside each fold so no test text leaks) reaches \textbf{$0.83$ $[0.73,0.90]$} on the
judge's SciFact pairs---above the judge's $0.80$, intervals overlapping---and $0.69$ $[0.54,0.81]$ on
Climate-FEVER, overlapping the judge's $[0.64,0.88]$ though the judge keeps a higher point estimate
($0.78$). On the judging step, then, the open--closed gap is smaller than the single-model rows suggest:
a combined open stack \emph{matches} the frontier judge on clean pairs and narrows the hard-task gap
to within sampling noise at $n=45$. The combiner uses public benchmark labels, not new in-domain
labels---the standard the rest of the pipeline holds to---and the strictly label-free part of the gain is
the majority vote. So the judging step does not require a closed model at all. What ensembling does not touch is claim extraction (validated separately in
\S\ref{sec:res-extraction}) and the joint-pipeline human audit, which we argue is a complementary check
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Self-preference does not explain the judge's accuracy}
\label{sec:res-selfpref}
The benchmark results rule out the central circularity threat. SciFact and Climate-FEVER claims and
evidence are written by humans, so a judge that labels them correctly is assessing the science, not
recognizing model-generated prose: self-preference \citep{panickssery2024llm} cannot manufacture an 80\%
match to human labels on human text (Fig.~\ref{fig:confusion}), and it cannot explain the judge's
accuracy on the policy-adjacent Climate-FEVER claims either. The three independently developed open
models perform the same task above the lexical floor on the same human-written benchmarks ($0.53$--$0.70$
on SciFact, $0.51$--$0.67$ on Climate-FEVER; Table~\ref{tab:validation}), confirming that the task is
tractable for models that did not generate the text. We do \emph{not}, however, lean on the panel as an
independent guarantee against self-preference: independently \emph{developed} is not independently
\emph{erring}---models trained on overlapping corpora and aligned by similar methods share systematic
tendencies, and the panel's agreement with the gold is itself only modest ($\kappa=0.28$--$0.44$ against
the gold, across both benchmarks). The
decisive control is the human-written benchmark text, which self-preference cannot explain; the panel
corroborates rather than guarantees.

This also reframes the apparent judge--NLI disagreement on the policy corpus. There the judge and NLI
agree only weakly ($\kappa=0.15$--$0.21$), which one might read as a problem for the judge. The
benchmarks show the opposite: it is NLI, not the judge, that is unreliable on policy-like text. Zero-shot
NLI agrees with the judge moderately on clean SciFact pairs ($\kappa=0.46$--$0.48$) but drops to near the
lexical floor on contested Climate-FEVER claims (accuracy $0.49$--$0.51$ against a $0.34$ floor;
$\kappa=0.23$--$0.27$ with the human gold). The weak policy agreement is therefore a property of strict
entailment under relevance retrieval---an abstract seldom \emph{entails} a lay claim even when it
substantiates it---identified \emph{against human labels} as NLI's failure, not the judge's. This is
exactly why we anchor estimation on the validated judge and treat NLI as a surrogate to be corrected
(\S\ref{sec:methods-ppi}), not as a free-standing measure.

\subsection{The judge transfers to application-like pairs: validation on retrieved-evidence claims}
\label{sec:res-application-validation}
The standing objection to our validation is that SciFact and Climate-FEVER pair a claim with evidence
\emph{curated to be about it}, whereas the application pairs a claim with \emph{retrieved} evidence that
may be off-topic---so benchmark accuracy might not transfer. We answer this with a third benchmark whose
pair structure matches the application and which already carries human labels, so no in-domain coding is
required. AVeriTeC \citep{schlichtkrull2023averitec} comprises real-world claims fact-checked by fifty
organisations, each paired with evidence \emph{retrieved from the open web}---the same noisy, multi-source
pairing the application produces---and labelled \textsc{supported}/\textsc{refuted}/\textsc{not-enough-evidence}
by trained annotators who agree at $\kappa=0.62$. On a class-balanced $90$-pair sample, the judge reaches
accuracy $0.83$ and macro-$F_1$ $0.83$, agreeing with the human verdicts at $\kappa=0.75$ against
AVeriTeC's own pairwise human inter-annotator agreement of $0.62$. The cross-developer panel again clears the
lexical/NLI floor ($0.59$--$0.66$ vs $0.54$), so the result is not one model's idiosyncrasy. The judge's
errors concentrate ($9$ of $15$) in the \textsc{not-enough-evidence} boundary---the class with the lowest
human agreement, which AVeriTeC re-annotated most---so the judge is uncertain exactly where the humans
are. One honest caveat on the comparison: the judge's $\kappa$ is measured against the \emph{adjudicated}
gold, whereas the human figure is \emph{pairwise} inter-annotator agreement, and agreeing with a settled
gold is the easier quantity; the comparison should be read in the alternative-annotator spirit
\citep{calderon2025alttest}---is the model inside the band of human disagreement---rather than as two
identical numbers. Taken together (Fig.~\ref{fig:band}), across the spectrum from curated pairs (SciFact) to
retrieved-evidence pairs (Climate-FEVER) to real-world web-retrieved pairs (AVeriTeC), the judge sits
\emph{within the band of human disagreement}, including on the two benchmarks whose pair structure matches
the application. The ``curated pairs only'' gap is, on this evidence, substantially narrowed---on the very
pair structure the application produces---though AVeriTeC differs from the application in domain (general
fact-checking) and in retrieval noise, so we say narrowed, not closed.

\subsection{Validating the extraction step against human labels, without human coders}
\label{sec:res-extraction}
Extraction is the pipeline's first step and was its last unvalidated one. Three checks, none requiring
new human coding, close that gap (Fig.~\ref{fig:extraction}).

\emph{The extractor identifies check-worthy claims at the published state of the art.} The decision the
extractor makes---is a sentence an empirical claim that needs a reference?---is the fact-checking task of
check-worthiness detection, for which ClaimBuster supplies ${\sim}23$k human-labelled US-debate sentences
\citep{hassan2017claimbuster}. On a class-balanced sample the frontier extractor matches the human labels
at accuracy $0.77$ and $F_1$ $0.74$, comparable to the CheckThat! shared-task state of the art of
${\sim}0.70$ $F_1$ \citep{barron2020checkthat} (with the caveat that our sample is balanced whereas that
figure is on the natural, imbalanced distribution), with high precision ($0.83$) and more conservative
recall ($0.67$): it rarely flags rhetoric as a claim and misses some borderline human-check-worthy
sentences---exactly the behaviour the ``needs a scientific reference'' criterion implies. Its agreement
with the human labels, Cohen's $\kappa=0.53$, is \emph{moderate}, not a ceiling; we did not compute
ClaimBuster's own inter-annotator agreement, so we claim state-of-the-art performance, not human parity.
The cross-developer panel performs the same task above the lexical floor (Phi-3.5 $F_1$ $0.63$ vs $0.56$
for TF--IDF), so this is task competence, not self-preference.

\emph{Extracted claims are faithful to their source.} The gravest failure---fabricating a claim the
document does not make---is rare: $95.2\%$ of the $785$ extracted policy claims are entailed by their own
source release under NLI ($95.3\%$ Democratic, $93.2\%$ Republican; mean maximum entailment $0.92$).
Inspecting the lowest-entailment cases, they are overwhelmingly paraphrases or claims spread across
several sentences that the entailment model misses, not inventions. Extraction therefore adds little
hallucination on top of the judging it feeds.

\emph{Yield: balanced per content-bearing release, but unequal unconditionally.} We separate two
quantities that are easily conflated. \emph{Conditional} on a release that yields any claims, the
extractor returns $7.1$ claims per Democratic release versus $7.3$ per Republican---balanced, so the
\emph{per-claim} machine behaviour does not differ by party. \emph{Unconditionally}, across all
AI-flagged releases, the yields are $2.9$ (Democratic) versus $0.9$ (Republican): Republican AI releases
far more often yield \emph{zero} claims, because they are procedural bill announcements with no empirical
content the extractor correctly declines (the same precision the check-worthiness test confirms). So
extraction does not bias the per-claim comparison, but it does mean the Republican corroboration arm is
thin ($11$ empirical claims with retrievable references), so although its rate is indistinguishable from
the Democratic rate, we read the cross-party \emph{comparison} as suggestive rather than a tested partisan
effect and rest the partisan finding on engagement volume. With judging
benchmark-validated (on curated \emph{and} retrieved-evidence pairs, \S\ref{sec:res-application-validation}),
retrieval open and verifiable, and extraction checked for faithfulness, precision, and yield, what the
design still forecloses is a full in-domain human audit of the \emph{joint} pipeline---which we argue
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}) is a complementary check rather than the decisive gate, given the judge's
validation on application-structure pairs.

\subsection{Reproducible, verifiable, model-guided retrieval}
\label{sec:res-retrieval}
Retrieval must surface, for each claim, science that is genuinely about the claim, and do so reproducibly
and from real sources rather than fabricated citations. The same frontier model that judges stance performs
this step: reading each claim, it emits a concise, claim-targeted query when the claim has a scientific core
and \emph{abstains} otherwise, and the free OpenAlex index (or, equivalently, Crossref) returns the
references, so every one is a real, dereferenceable record (Fig.~\ref{fig:retrieval}). We show this recovers
relevant evidence at high yield, abstains appropriately, and---critically---that the relevance judgment
itself matches human relevance labels.

\emph{Targeting.} On the 30 empirical claims in a balanced 50-claim sample, model-guided queries surface a
genuinely relevant reference---as assessed by the same benchmark-validated judge, because NLI underdetects
relevance on policy text---in the top three for $29$ of $30$ claims ($0.97$), versus $12$ of $30$ ($0.40$)
for naive keyword search over the identical index. The naive failures are not subtle: for ``sea level is
expected to climb\ldots by 2100'' keyword search returns papers on sea stars, a rock-climbing vision aid,
and soft robotics (matching ``sea'' and ``climb''); for the strained electrical grid it returns the
\emph{Gaia} space mission; for affordable housing for foster youth it returns Alzheimer's-disease facts.
Model guidance instead retrieves sea-level-rise projections, electric-vehicle charging grid-impact
studies, and longitudinal foster-youth outcomes.

\emph{Relevance is human-anchored, not self-graded.} The relevance yield above is judged by the same model
that wrote the queries, which invites the objection that the judgment is self-assessed. We close this the
way we closed the stance question---by transfer from human labels. Relevance is the one judgment on which
humans agree well (unlike stance on contested claims), and SciFact's human evidence annotations supply a
ready test: the cited abstract is on-topic for a claim, a random abstract is not. On $120$ such pairs the
model's relevance judgment matches the human labels at $96\%$ accuracy with \emph{perfect precision}
($1.00$; it never calls an off-topic paper relevant) and $0.92$ recall, and the cross-developer panel again
clears the lexical floor (\texttt{relevance\_validation.py}). We are deliberately careful about what this
does and does not establish. The negatives here are random abstracts, so the test exercises the
\emph{easy} discrimination---telling a cited paper from an unrelated one---and the perfect precision means
the model is reliable on exactly that case, ruling out the worst failure mode. It does \emph{not} certify
the \emph{hard} discrimination the application actually meets: telling an on-point paper from a
topically-adjacent one, the data-center paper that is not about water, which is where the application's
retrieval misses live (\S\ref{sec:res-application}). The honest indicator of that hard boundary is already in
our validation: it is AVeriTeC's retrieved-but-insufficient (\textsc{not-enough-evidence}) class, on which
the judge's errors concentrate ($9$ of $15$, \S\ref{sec:res-application-validation}). The complete
statement is therefore that the model rejects obvious mismatches reliably ($96\%$, precision $1.00$) but is
uncertain precisely on the retrieved-but-adjacent boundary; a cited-versus-same-topic relevance test is the
clean way to close that boundary directly, and we flag it as the next step.

\emph{Abstention.} Naive keyword search cannot decline: it returned references for $19$ of the $20$
non-empirical claims---legal rulings (\emph{De La Torre v.\ CashCall}), legislative-procedure statements,
gas-price and drug-seizure statistics---pairing each with broad papers a judge must then sift. The model
abstained on all $20$, emitting a query only where a scientific literature exists---the selectivity a
keyword search cannot supply, reproduced here transparently and committed as data.

\emph{Verifiability.} Because the references are records returned by a bibliographic index rather than text
a model generated, every one is real and dereferenceable: both arms ground about $90\%$ of references in a
DOI ($0.89$ model-guided, $0.91$ naive), and the rest are indexed works without a registered DOI, not
fabrications---eliminating the citation-fabrication failure mode of asking a model to name references.
Running the validated judge on the best on-topic model-retrieved reference gives an end-to-end corroboration
read from a fully reproducible pipeline: the literature supports $24$ of $30$ empirical claims, refutes
none, and is silent on the rest ($0.80$). We offer this as a small-sample demonstration, not a population
estimate---$n=30$ and stance is read from abstracts. One honest miss travels with it: neither arm found
support for ``$80\%$ of California vehicle trips are under ten miles,'' a travel-survey statistic with no
referent in the article literature the index covers. Model-guided retrieval improves targeting and
verifiability; it does not turn every legislative claim into one with a retrievable scientific source.

\subsection{Application: how often the nearest literature corroborates legislators' AI claims}
\label{sec:res-application}
We apply the pipeline to a single corpus of California State Assembly member press releases spanning
2015--2026, assembled by scraping both caucus archives from their sitemaps and kept current through June
2026 (\S\ref{sec:methods-corpus}). What the instrument measures is specific and we name it precisely:
legislators do not cite papers, so the instrument \emph{supplies} the evidence, retrieving the
topically-nearest literature and asking whether it corroborates each extracted claim. It therefore measures
claim--literature \emph{corroboration}, not observed evidence \emph{use}.

\emph{The corpus.} Full-text detection flags the AI-related releases, from which the pipeline extracts
$829$ empirical claims ($785$ Democratic, $44$ Republican) over $130$ releases. The claims describe the
live AI-policy agenda---algorithmic decision-making and bias, companion-chatbot child safety, data-center
energy and water demand, deepfakes and synthetic media, misinformation, and AI in health and the
workforce---and attention to AI rises sharply from 2023 onward, tracking the public arrival of generative
AI (Fig.~\ref{fig:descriptives}). Yield per release is broadly flat with a mode at the ten-claim extraction
cap.

\emph{Engagement is large and partisan.} Across the AI-related corpus, Democratic members engage with AI
more than Republicans on every margin (Fig.~\ref{fig:engage}): they mention AI in $4.8\%$ of releases
versus $2.6\%$, and when they do they say more---$3.3$ AI statements per document on average versus
$1.6$. These are descriptive aggregates over an overwhelmingly Democratic chamber in which a few prolific
offices can drive intensity; we report them without member-clustered intervals and so read the gap as
descriptive, not as a tested effect (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\emph{How often the nearest literature corroborates the claims.} For each extracted claim the model-guided
pipeline retrieves the topically-nearest literature and the benchmark-validated judge assesses support;
because the cheap NLI surrogate is near-constant on policy text, the prediction-powered estimate reduces in
practice to the judge's mean on the labelled sample (\S\ref{sec:methods-ppi}). Over a
stratified sample spanning the full corpus---$45$ Democratic claims and all $11$ Republican empirical
claims for which the index returned references---the judge corroborates $34$, refutes none, and is silent
on the rest: an end-to-end corroboration rate of $\mathbf{0.61}$ ($95\%$ CI $[0.48,0.72]$), counting the
cases where retrieval failed to surface relevant evidence as non-corroborations. The rate is similar across
parties (Democratic $0.60$ $[0.46,0.73]$; Republican $0.64$ $[0.35,0.85]$, on a thin eleven-claim base), so
the robust partisan asymmetry is in the \emph{volume} of AI engagement, not in how well each party's claims
are corroborated (Fig.~\ref{fig:corr}). Three honest qualifications travel with the
number. First, it is
the quantity in which the model's stages stack---LLM-extracted claims scored by an LLM judge---so although
each stage is independently validated against human labels (extraction, \S\ref{sec:res-extraction};
retrieval relevance, \S\ref{sec:res-retrieval}; stance, \S\ref{sec:res-application-validation}), the
\emph{joint} behaviour on this corpus is not separately human-audited. Second, the construct is only as
sharp as human agreement allows: when trained annotators agree only moderately on whether evidence supports
a contested claim (Climate-FEVER $\alpha=0.33$), a corroboration rate carries construct ambiguity, not
merely sampling uncertainty. Third, about two in five claims fall to \textsc{silent}, many of them
retrieval misses where the free index returned a topically-adjacent paper rather than the specific study a
claim invokes; the rate is thus computed among the claims the index served, with misses counted against it,
and is read as a lower bound for that reason and because relevance-seeking retrieval does not search for
disconfirming evidence---and press releases are advocacy, so a high rate also reflects selective citation
of favourable science as much as evidence quality.

\subsection{Reproducibility, retrieval precision, and domain transfer}
\label{sec:res-robustness}
\emph{Retrieval precision governs the level---which is why \S\ref{sec:res-retrieval} matters.} The
corroboration \emph{level} is governed at least as much by retrieval precision as by the judge. Naive
OpenAlex keyword search returns broad, topically-related papers rather than the specific study a claim
invokes, so across \emph{every} judge---both NLI classifiers and all three open panel models---nearly all
pairs are labelled \textsc{silent} and corroboration rates collapse to a few percent (0.6--13\%). The fix
is therefore not a better judge but better retrieval, and the model-guided retrieval of
\S\ref{sec:res-retrieval} supplies it \emph{without} a paid service: it lifts relevance yield from $0.40$
to $0.97$ while keeping the evidence base fully reproducible and DOI-grounded, the queries committed as
data. The model-guided retriever is therefore reproducible and claim-targeted, using the one frontier model
already in the loop rather than a separately trained dense retriever, and it grounds $\sim$$90\%$ of
references in a DOI with every reference a real, dereferenceable record. The judge's \emph{labels}, and the
retrieval \emph{queries}, are committed and frozen, so the downstream analysis is reproducible exactly as
frozen human labels would make it, even though regenerating either requires the same frontier model. What retrieval precision does \emph{not} change
is the directional caveat: the pipeline seeks \emph{relevant} rather than deliberately \emph{disconfirming}
evidence, so corroboration rates remain an upper and refute rates a lower bound. \emph{Domain transfer.}
The pipeline's front end applies unchanged
to a separate corpus of 5{,}131 K--12 education-policy releases, 611 (11.9\%) of which are
education-related, recovering an analogous partisan asymmetry in attention (Fig.~\ref{fig:edu}):
Democrats issued 80 releases on social-emotional learning and student mental health versus 1 for
Republicans, and 38 on school safety versus 4. The measurement instrument is thus not specific to AI or
to one retrieval engine.
