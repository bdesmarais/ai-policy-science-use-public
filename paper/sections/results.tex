\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Engagement with AI is large and partisan}
Democrats engage with AI far more than Republicans on every margin (Figure~\ref{fig:engage}).
Democratic members mention AI in 4.8\% of their press releases versus 2.6\% for Republicans, and
when they do, they say more about it: Democratic AI documents contain 3.3 AI statements on average
versus 1.6 for Republicans. Across the 303 AI documents the pipeline retrieves a substantial and
accessible body of candidate evidence---614 claim--reference pairs with abstracts, 87\% of them
open-access, drawn from venues led by arXiv and concentrated in the years since 2015. The raw
material for evidence-based AI policy clearly exists; the question is whether legislators' claims
are borne out by it.

\subsection{Generative and discriminative validators disagree by method}
A first result is that the two \emph{kinds} of validator disagree sharply (Table~\ref{tab:agree},
Figure~\ref{fig:agree}). The two NLI classifiers, which share the discriminative entailment paradigm,
agree strongly with each other ($\kappa = 0.66$ over all 614 pairs), but each agrees only weakly with
the generative LLM judge ($\kappa = 0.21$ and $0.15$ over the 120 gold pairs). The mechanism is that
strict textual entailment between a study's abstract and a press-release claim is almost always
classified ``neutral''---hence \textsc{silent}---even when the study substantiates the claim's
substance, because the abstract rarely restates the claim in entailing language: our zero-shot NLI
setup labels 76\% of pairs \textsc{silent}, the LLM judge only 23\%. Two cautions are essential.
\emph{(i)} This is \emph{our zero-shot NLI configuration}, not a fair representative of automated claim
verification; strong systems on tasks like SciFact \citep{wadden2020fact} fine-tune and select
rationale sentences and do not equate ``non-entailment'' with ``no support,'' so we cannot claim that
NLI \emph{as a class} under-detects support. \emph{(ii)} High \emph{within}-method agreement reflects a
shared property of the two NLI models---which could be shared conservatism \emph{or} shared error---and
does not establish which validator is correct; absent a human anchor, neither does the disagreement. We
report the disagreement as a flag, not as proof that the LLM judge is right \citep{calderon2025alttest}.

\subsection{Under the LLM judge, support rates look high---but this is a conditional estimate}
Relative to the naive NLI surrogate (0.21 for Democratic, 0.11 for Republican claims), anchoring on
the LLM judge's 120 gold labels and applying PPI (Eq.~\ref{eq:ppi}) roughly triples the estimates, to
\textbf{0.71} (95\% CI $[0.57, 0.84]$) for Democrats and \textbf{0.54} ($[0.41, 0.67]$) for
Republicans (Table~\ref{tab:support}, Figure~\ref{fig:support}); the judge's raw rates (0.70 and 0.57)
are similar, as they must be, since the judge \emph{is} the anchor. We deliberately do not headline
this as ``most claims are supported.'' A roughly threefold gap between surrogate and judge is far
larger than is typical when a surrogate is competent (in the PPI/DSL literature surrogates are usually
80--90\% accurate and corrections are modest), and we cannot, without a human anchor, attribute it to
NLI error rather than to the judge \emph{over}-calling support---a live possibility given self-preference
in an all-LLM loop (\S\ref{sec:methods}). The level is also inflated by construction: retrieval is
relevance-seeking with no disconfirming search, and some ``supporting'' references are bill text or
government pages rather than peer-reviewed studies. We therefore read the level as ``high under our
judge,'' not as an established fact about evidence use.

What is more robust is \emph{comparative} and \emph{negative}. The large partisan gap implied by the
naive NLI estimate (Democratic claims nearly twice as likely to be ``supported'') does not survive a
better judge: under the LLM judge the rates are 0.71 vs.\ 0.54 with overlapping intervals. The apparent
partisan asymmetry in evidentiary quality is thus an artifact of a poorly-configured instrument; the
asymmetry that is robust across instruments is the \emph{volume and intensity} of AI engagement
(Fig.~\ref{fig:engage}), not the support for the claims.

\paragraph{A robustness check that reinforces caution.} Two features of the LLM judge's own labels
underline why the level should not be over-read. First, conditional on the judge deeming a pair relevant
(non-\textsc{silent}), the support rate is very high---$0.86$ (D) and $0.79$ (R)---so almost every
relevant claim--reference pair is judged ``support.'' Second, \textsc{refute} is nearly absent: just $6$
of $120$ gold pairs ($5\%$; one Democratic, five Republican). A pipeline that almost never surfaces
disconfirming evidence is what relevance-only retrieval plus a possibly self-preferring judge would
produce, and it is not what a balanced assessment of contestable legislative claims should look like. We
read this less as ``legislators are well-supported'' than as ``this autonomous configuration is
structurally disposed to find support''---the central reason we present the rates as conditional.

\subsection{Domain transfer: education policy}
The pipeline's front end transfers to a different polarized domain. On a separate corpus of 5{,}131
education-policy press releases, 611 (11.9\%) are education-related, with the same kind of partisan
asymmetry in attention (Figure~\ref{fig:edu}): Democrats issued 80 releases on social-emotional
learning and student mental health versus 1 for Republicans, and 38 on school safety versus 4.
These asymmetries in \emph{which} topics receive attention motivate extending the full
support-assessment analysis to education in future work.
